More Info

The different ‘Standards’ options provided by the application were selected by simply looking at OSHA regulations and utilizing different levels that they specified. The so-called ‘Engineering standard’ selection sets the permissible exposure limit (PEL) to 90, similarly the ‘Hearing conservation program’ selection sets the PEL to 80. PEL is simply the level of sound that is safe for a person to experience when their noise dosage is converted to an 8 hour time weighted average.

Snippet explaining our base values (taken from “Noise Exposure: Explanation of OSHA and NIOSH Safe-Exposure Limits and the Importance of Noise Dosimetry”):

OSHA uses a criterion level of 90 dB, a threshold level of 80 dB, and an exchange rate of 5 dB. An OSHA 100% noise dose is 90 dB for 8 hours, 95 dB for 4 hours, 100 dB for 2 hours, and so on (OSHA decreases the PEL to 85 dB for 8 hours if the employee has a documented threshold shift; see 29CFR 1910.95 for additional details).

NIOSH uses a criterion level of 85 dB, a threshold level of 75 dB, and an exchange rate of 3 dB. A NIOSH 100% noise dose is 85 dB for 8 hours, 88 dB for 4 hours, 91dB for 2 hours, and so on.

Another snippet from OSHA’s website that explains our equation for the percent dosage calculation code:

When the workshift noise exposure is composed of two or more periods of noise at different levels, the total noise dose over the work day is given by:

D = 100(C1 / T1 + C2 / T2 + Cn / Tn),

where Cn indicates the total time of exposure at a specific noise level, and Tn indicates the reference duration for that level as given by Table G-16a.

We use a formula on the provided Noise Reduction Rating (NRR) to reduce the advertised protection to a more realistic level that might be experienced in less than ideal conditions.
